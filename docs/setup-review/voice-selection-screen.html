Design Rationale — Split Control in Remote Setup

This screen embodies a deliberate split of control: the patient physically operates the device, but the clinician makes the clinical decision. This split exists because (a) the clinician has professional knowledge of which voice characteristics aid LSVT compliance for a given patient, and (b) the patient may not have preferences or may be easily influenced by framing — putting the choice in the clinician's hands removes that cognitive burden.

In practice, the interaction is verbal: the clinician says "please play Voice A… now play Voice B… okay, let's use Voice B." The patient taps cards to audition, then taps Voice B's card to select it. The UI never asks the patient to choose — it only asks them to play and then confirm.

The auditioning state uses outlined play buttons (not filled) to signal that pressing play is a low-stakes action. The selection state uses a filled teal confirmation button at the bottom to signal the commitment step. The two visual registers (outlined vs filled) map directly to audition vs commit.

Voice labels are abstract (A, B, C) rather than descriptive names like "Emma" or "Standard Female" — this prevents patients from picking based on label preference rather than auditory experience, and keeps the clinician's verbal instruction unambiguous.

Additional Annotations

  • Voice card tap area: The full card is tappable (not just the play button) in the auditioning state, to maximise touch target size for elderly users.
  • No "Back" button shown: Back navigation will exist in the final design; omitted here to focus review on the core interaction pattern.
  • Three voices: Three options were chosen as the maximum — more options add cognitive load with diminishing clinical benefit. The number may be reduced to two in the final build.
  • Voice metadata ("Warm, calm tone"): Short descriptor aids the clinician in directing the patient verbally ("the calm sounding one") if a number alone isn't sufficient. Copy is placeholder — final wording TBD.
